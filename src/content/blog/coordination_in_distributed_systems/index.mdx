## Why Coordination is Hard in Distributed Environments
Distributed systems may seem great in theory. Multiple computers working together so smoothly that you would think they were one computer.
However, once distributed systems have networks separating them from each other, things quickly become confusing. In addition to delay, lost communications,
duplicate communications, and out-of-order communications there will be uncertainty. It is this uncertainty which will introduce many problems such as
the Two Generals Problem.

If you could imagine coordinating a meeting with someone who had an unreliable mail system (i.e., messages sent at random times), you would send a
confirmation message, however, you would never know whether they received your confirmation. They would then send you a reply message; however, again,
you would have no idea whether your reply was received. This "indefinite" loop of doubt has a very real correlation in the world of distributed systems
where nodes need to agree upon some type of common state.

Coordination in distributed computing refers to the act of achieving agreement amongst all nodes (computers) in a distributed environment.
Coordination occurs when all nodes reach consensus regarding a value or decision. Consensus is typically used for critical activities within a
distributed environment including but not limited to committing transactions to databases and verifying blocks in a blockchain.
While consensus is obviously a fundamental component of distributed environments, achieving reliable consensus under conditions where communication
is not guaranteed is extremely challenging.

As distributed environments grow in size, the challenges related to reliability also increase. As the number of nodes in a distributed environment
increases exponentially with each node added to the environment, the number of possible communication paths also increases exponentially.
Additionally, as the number of possible communication paths increases, so do the potential failure points associated with these communication paths.
Latency, packet loss, and network partitioning also create fragility in the process of coordination.
Therefore, while the problems discussed here may appear abstractly interesting and therefore simply academic exercises,
they represent significant limitations in what can actually be achieved through distributed systems.

## Real-World Examples of Distributed Failures
If you think distributed failure sounds far fetched, there's no shortage of examples in real world applications.
Consider a bank's payment processing when two servers confirm a transfer and yet one server does not.
This may also occur with a cloud service down where some parts of the system function correctly while other parts are shut down entirely.

Amazon has had several instances where communication errors caused cascaded failures among distributed application components.
Google's use of their product spanner and Apache Kafka have developed specific consensus protocols based on their knowledge of this type of problem.
Sending an e-mail is a very good example of the types of problems related to distributed failures.
Once sent, do your recipients know they received the message? Even if you receive a "sent" or "delivered" confirmation from your mail service provider,
that doesn't mean they opened it. Scaling up to tens of millions of transactions/second and the implications are significant.

## The Two Generals Problem
The Two Generals Problem may seem very simple yet has great depth. Think of two generals who are stationed on opposite sides of hills,
each preparing to attack a city located between them. In order for both generals to have success with their simultaneous attack against the city,
they must attack at the same time; otherwise, one will lose.

There is only one method which allows the generals to synchronize their actions: to send messages through the valley. However,
because of the danger from the enemy in the valley, all or part of a messenger's message(s) could potentially be lost. Therefore,
neither general knows if his message (or her message), and/or the acknowledgment of that message were actually delivered to the other general.

For example, suppose General A sent a message: "Attack at Dawn." General B then sends back a confirmation: "Okay I got it!" Now assume that
this confirmation was lost. Since General A cannot confirm that General B received General A's original message, he therefore may send another
acknowledgment of General B's acknowledgment and so on. This situation would create an infinite loop. Regardless of the number of messages exchanged,
General A will never know if General B received the last message.

## Why Reliable Communication Fails
The basic idea behind the two generals problem illustrates how it's impossible to achieve reliable communication in a communications system
with unreliable connections. Even if you have high-reliability connection for most of your transmissions there is still the potential for a
small amount of unreliability which can create uncertainty as to whether or not a message was delivered reliably.

Reliability in today's network architecture is based upon many different methods. One example of this is TCP protocol.
The TCP protocol uses a method called acknowledgment/retransmission to assure reliability.
However, even though this assures that the majority of the data will reach its destination, it does not provide complete assurance
that all data sent will be received by the intended recipient. In fact, the best that TCP can do is provide some level of probable reliability.
Therefore, no matter what measures are taken to increase the reliability of data transmission over a network, there will always be a remote possibility of failure.

The limitations imposed by lack of complete reliability when using networks to communicate, severely limits our ability
to design and build completely reliable distributed systems. Distributed systems therefore need to be able to account for lack of complete reliability.
As such, developers and designers typically implement various forms of redundancy, timeouts, and retries to help approximate
reliability within their systems rather than attempting to establish true reliability.

Practically speaking, engineers know that perfect coordination is never possible; however, they also understand that they
should strive for building systems that perform well "enough" during typical usage scenarios. As a result of this understanding,
many systems (distributed) operate on an eventually consistent basis. While these systems may not consistently produce the correct
output at exactly the same moment in time, they tend to converge toward consistency as needed.

## Mathematical Insight into Impossibility
The Two Generals Problem is a classic example of an impossibility proof from the standpoint of mathematics.
In this case, the problem demonstrates that there is no way to develop a method of communication that guarantees both sides agree when
the channel through which they communicate is unreliable and each side requires confirmation before making a decision.

Inductive reasoning underlies the proof. Since the final transmission can be dropped, it does not provide assurance that both will
reach agreement. However, since the last transmission does not assure agreement, neither do prior transmissions.
Inductive reasoning proceeds infinitely backwards (i.e., forever), demonstrating that no finite number of communications can result in absolute assurance.

As such, the implications of the Two Generals Problem were felt throughout the field of Distributed Computing.
Notably, the FLP Impossibility Theorem resulted from these implications.
This theorem states that in any Asynchronous System where at least one process fails, obtaining Consensus is impossible.

Therefore, while the Two Generals Problem may seem as though it is nothing but a simplistic representation of a common military strategy,
it illustrates a fundamental truth: some problems associated with Distributed Systems are fundamentally incapable of having a "perfect" solution.

## Impact on Network Protocol Design
In a fundamental way, the Two Generals Problem affects how engineering teams develop their communications protocols.
Rather than striving toward complete reliability (which is not possible mathematically), protocol developers concentrate upon
fault tolerant designs with probabilities of success guaranteed.

TCP, as another example, provides assurances regarding proper receipt of information using acknowledgment packets,
re-transmission of lost or damaged packets, and packet sequencing numbers. However, it does so under the assumption of some delay
in delivery and at some point will deliver all packets successfully. It cannot provide absolute assurance of receiving the correct packets every time.

The implications of this lack of absolute assurance has significant influence upon how distributed database systems are designed.
Cassandra and DynamoDB both utilize eventual consistency models. Eventually consistent models rely upon gradual synchronization of
nodes over time rather than requiring immediate consistency. The decision to use eventual consistency models is a direct reflection
of the impossibility established by the Two Generals Problem.

Idempotence (ensuring that repeated execution of an operation results in the same outcome) is another important consideration.
Because systems may execute operations repeatedly as a result of uncertainty, ensuring idempotence ensures that multiple executions do not produce additional problems.

## Practical Workarounds in Modern Systems
So what do actual systems do to address this completely unsolvable problem? Systems cheat, but they are very creative in their cheating.

Instead of achieving absolute confidence that some event has occurred or will occur, systems use:

- **Timeouts**: If a system does not receive a reply from another system within a predetermined amount of time (or period) it assumes that the other system has failed.
- **Redundancy**: A message can be sent by a single source via multiple paths to ensure that if one path fails, the message will still arrive at its destination.
- **Consensus** Protocols: Instead of needing all systems to agree on something before proceeding; consensus protocols need most of the systems (a majority) to agree on something before proceeding.

The above methods do not remove uncertainty; instead, they work to manage uncertainty. For example, in distributed database environments, a
write operation would be accepted (and thus considered successful) when the majority of nodes that were involved in the write operation
acknowledged receipt of the write operation. By using this approach, a system could minimize the chance of inconsistent data being written
into the database environment; however, the system would not have to achieve complete and/or perfect communication in order to accomplish this goal.

A second method used to resolve this type of problem is manual intervention. In many cases, where critical systems exist; the occurrence
of an unresolved state may result in a manual review process. This is obviously not practical for all types of problems; however,
this provides an important insight: sometimes the best way to solve a problem is not by implementing additional automated processes,
but rather by providing humans a role in the decision-making process.

In conclusion, the Two Generals Problem is a sobering reminder that **systems should be designed with failure in mind**, as
opposed to designing systems based upon the assumption that failures will never occur.
